This page introduces **יסודות פון אידישער גראמאטיק און אויסלייג** — Foundations of Yiddish Grammar and Spelling — a handbook for Yiddish writers, editors, and proofreaders who want to write clear, consistent, and beautiful Yiddish.

## What this handbook is

יסודות פון אידישער גראמאטיק (א האנטבוך פאר אידישע שרייבער און מגיהים) is a concise reference that lays out the rules of Yiddish grammar and spelling in plain, accessible language. It is aimed at anyone who writes or edits Yiddish — whether you produce reading materials, books, publications, or Torah-related works.

In recent years, the field of Yiddish writing has grown significantly. New reading materials, books, journals, and even Torah works in Yiddish continue to appear. At the same time, grammar instruction in Yiddish has lagged behind: children in chadorim and girls' schools learn Hebrew grammar and the grammar of local secular languages, but almost no one teaches the rules of Yiddish itself. This handbook sets out to fill that gap.

## Who this handbook is for

This handbook is written for:

- **Yiddish writers** who want to produce polished, consistent prose
- **Editors and proofreaders (מגיהים)** who need a reliable reference when reviewing Yiddish texts
- **Anyone** who wishes to deepen their command of written Yiddish

You do not need to be a linguist to use it. The rules are presented as concisely and simply as possible so that every interested reader can apply them.

## Why grammar rules matter

You might wonder: does it really matter whether you follow grammar rules, as long as readers understand what you wrote? And who decides what the rules even are?

The short answer is that grammar rules exist in every language as a natural phenomenon — not as arbitrary impositions. Native Yiddish speakers already follow many rules without thinking about them. The handbook addresses the cases where usage is less settled and where inconsistency disrupts the reading experience.

One of the most important reasons to follow consistent rules is **uniformity within a single text**. When the same word appears spelled two different ways within one article, it disrupts the flow for the reader — regardless of which spelling you choose. Having a clear standard means you spend less time deliberating over individual choices and more time writing.

## What the handbook covers

The handbook addresses the core areas of Yiddish grammar and spelling, including:

- Parts of speech and their forms (nouns, pronouns, verbs, adjectives)
- Case usage — for example, when to write **מיך** versus **מיר**
- Spelling conventions and letter choices
- Standards for consistent, readable Yiddish prose

## A note on emphasis

<Note>
  Yiddish typography does not use italic type for emphasis. Throughout this handbook, **bold** is used instead of italics whenever a Yiddish word or phrase needs to be highlighted or stressed.
</Note>

## How to contribute

This handbook is an open project. If you find an error, want to suggest an addition, or would like to improve the wording of any rule, you are welcome to contribute.
