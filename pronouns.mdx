---
title: "Yiddish Pronouns: Personal and Impersonal Forms"
description: "A guide to Yiddish personal pronouns — subject and object forms, the impersonal מען, how pronouns combine with verbs, and common contractions."
---

Pronouns — **פראנאמען** — stand in for people and sometimes things when you don't want to name them directly. In Yiddish, pronouns interact closely with verbs: they determine which conjugated form of a verb you use, and they can even fuse with a verb to form a contraction.

## Personal pronouns

The personal pronouns of Yiddish are:

| Pronoun | Person / Notes |
|---------|---------------|
| **איך** | I (first person singular) |
| **דו** | you (second person singular, familiar) |
| **ער** | he (third person masculine singular) |
| **זי** | she (third person feminine singular); also they (third person plural, variant) |
| **עס** | it (third person neuter) |
| **מיר** | we (first person plural) |
| **איר** | you (second person plural; also formal singular, equivalent to "Sie" in German) |
| **זיי** | they (third person plural) |
| **עטס** | you all (second person plural, in certain dialects and registers) |
| **מען** | one / you / they (impersonal third person) |

<Info>
**איר** serves double duty: it is both the second-person plural ("you all") and the formal second-person singular — the polite form used when addressing a single person with respect. Context makes the meaning clear.
</Info>

## How pronouns determine verb forms

Every pronoun triggers a specific verb ending in the present tense. Using **זאגן** (to say) as the model:

| Pronoun | Verb form | Full phrase |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| איך | **זאג** | איך זאג |
| דו | **זאגסט** | דו זאגסט |
| ער / זי / עס / מען | **זאגט** | ער זאגט |
| מיר / זיי | **זאגן** | מיר זאגן |
| איר | **זאגט** | איר זאגט |
| עטס | **זאגטס** | עטס זאגטס |

Any noun subject — a name, a thing, any third-person reference — follows the same rule as **ער / זי / עס / מען** and takes the **-ט** ending.

## Subject and object forms

Like many languages, Yiddish distinguishes between the **subject form** (nominative, used when the pronoun is doing the action) and the **object form** (accusative/dative, used when the pronoun receives the action or follows a preposition).

| Subject form | Object form | Example |
|-------------|------------|---------|
| **איך** | **מיך** | ער זעט **מיך** — he sees me |
| **דו** | **דיך** | איך זע **דיך** — I see you |
| **ער** | **אים** | זי רעדט מיט **אים** — she speaks with him |
| **זי** | **איר** | ער גיט **איר** — he gives her |
| **עס** | **עס** | מיר נעמען **עס** — we take it |
| **מיר** | **אונדז** | זיי זעען **אונדז** — they see us |
| **איר** | **אייך** | ער רעדט צו **אייך** — he speaks to you |
| **זיי** | **זיי** | מיר שיקן **זיי** — we send them |

<Note>
The object form of **זי** (she) is **איר**, which looks identical to the subject pronoun **איר** (you/plural). Context and verb form clarify the meaning: **ער גיט איר** = he gives her; **איר גיט** = you give.
</Note>

## The impersonal pronoun מען

**מען** is the impersonal pronoun, equivalent to the generic "one," "you," or "they" in English — used when no specific person is named as the subject:

> **מ'זאגט** אז ס'איז ריכטיג — they say / one says / it is said that it is correct  
> **מ'קען** נישט קענען אלעס — you cannot know everything  
> **מען** זאגט, **מען** טוט, **מען** גייט

**מען** always takes the same verb form as **ער/זי/עס** (the third-person singular ending **-ט**): **מען זאגט, מען טוט, מען גייט**.

## Contractions with verbs

Pronouns frequently contract with the verbs that follow them. The most common contractions are with **איך** and **מען**:

| Full form | Contraction | Example |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| **איך האב** | **כ'האב** | **כ'האב** נישט געוואוסט |
| **איך בין** | **כ'בין** | **כ'בין** דא |
| **איך וועל** | **כ'וועל** | **כ'וועל** קומען |
| **מען זאגט** | **מ'זאגט** | **מ'זאגט** אז... |
| **מען האט** | **מ'האט** | **מ'האט** עס נישט באמערקט |

<Tip>
Contractions like **כ'האב** and **מ'זאגט** are a natural part of fluent Yiddish speech and writing. Using them makes your Yiddish sound more natural. The apostrophe marks where a vowel (or syllable) has been dropped.
</Tip>

## Pronouns in the past tense

In the past tense, pronouns determine the form of the auxiliary verb **האבן** or **זיין**, while the main verb stays fixed. Using **זאגן** as an example:

| Pronoun | Past tense |
|---------|-----------|
| איך | **האב** געזאגט |
| דו | **האסט** געזאגט |
| ער / זי / עס / מען | **האט** געזאגט |
| מיר / זיי | **האבן** געזאגט |
| עטס | **האטס** געזאגט |

For verbs that use **זיין** as auxiliary (e.g., **קומען, גיין, בלייבן**):

| Pronoun | Past tense |
|---------|-----------|
| איך | **בין** געקומען |
| דו | **ביסט** געקומען |
| ער / זי / עס / מען | **איז** געקומען |
| מיר / זיי | **זענען** געקומען |
| איר | **זענט** געקומען |
| עטס | **זענטס** געקומען |
