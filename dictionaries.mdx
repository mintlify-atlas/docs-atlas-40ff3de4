A good Yiddish dictionary is not just a translation tool — it is an indispensable grammar reference. When you write or proofread Yiddish, you will regularly need to verify the past tense form of a verb, the plural of a noun, or the gender of a noun. All of these are listed in a well-made Yiddish dictionary, and knowing how to read a dictionary entry will save you from common errors.

## What to look up in a dictionary

### Past tense forms of verbs

Yiddish verbs do not follow a single predictable pattern in the past tense. As this handbook explains, some verbs add **גע־** and **־ט**, others add only **גע־**, and others undergo a vowel change in the root — and there is no rule that tells you which category a given verb belongs to without prior knowledge.

> "אויב מ׳ווייסט נישט, דארף מען נאכקוקן אין א ווערטערבוך. אין די ווערטערביכער שטייען געווענליך אויסגערעכנט נעבן יעדן ווערב זייער לשון־עבר פארעם."
>
> — "If you don't know, you need to look it up in a dictionary. Dictionaries usually list the past tense form of each verb next to it."

Whenever you are uncertain about the past tense form of a verb, look it up. The dictionary entry will show the infinitive form — which is also how you search for the verb — alongside its past tense form (לשון־עבר פארעם).

<Tip>
Search for a verb in its **infinitive form** (e.g., שרייבן, לויפן, נעמען). The dictionary will then show you the past tense form so you can use it correctly.
</Tip>

### Plural forms of nouns

Yiddish noun plurals are not predictable from the singular form alone. Dictionaries list the plural form alongside each noun entry. Before writing the plural of any noun you are not certain about, confirm it in a dictionary.

### Gender of nouns

Yiddish nouns have grammatical gender: masculine (דער), feminine (די), or neuter (דאס). Gender determines which articles, adjective endings, and case forms to use. Dictionaries mark the gender of each noun — look for the definite article listed with the entry (דער, די, or דאס).

<Note>
Getting the gender of a noun wrong will produce incorrect adjective endings and article forms throughout a sentence. When you introduce an unfamiliar noun, check its gender in a dictionary first.
</Note>

## How to read a dictionary entry

A typical entry in a Yiddish dictionary provides the following information:

| Field | What it tells you | Example |
|-------|-------------------|---------|
| Headword (infinitive or nominative) | The base form of the word | שרייבן |
| Part of speech | Verb, noun, adjective, etc. | ווערב |
| Past tense form (for verbs) | The לשון־עבר פארעם | געשריבן |
| Gender marker (for nouns) | דער / די / דאס | דאס בוך |
| Plural form (for nouns) | The לשון־רבים פארעם | ביכער |
| Definition or translation | Meaning in the target language | to write |

When you look up a verb, find the past tense form in the entry and use that form exactly. Do not guess or construct it by analogy with a similar-sounding verb — Yiddish has enough irregular forms that analogy frequently fails.

## Dictionaries and this handbook

The grammatical rules in this handbook — covering verb conjugation, noun declension, adjective agreement, and more — give you the framework for building correct Yiddish sentences. Dictionaries supply the specific forms you plug into that framework.

Think of them as complementary tools:

- **This handbook** explains *how* verb tenses, noun plurals, and adjective endings work.
- **A dictionary** tells you *what* the specific form of a given word is.

<Info>
Even native Yiddish speakers occasionally consult a dictionary to confirm a past tense form or plural. Looking things up is not a sign of weakness — it is good writing practice.
</Info>

## Using a dictionary for proofreading

When proofreading Yiddish text, make it a habit to verify any verb past tense form or noun plural that seems unusual. If the author has written a past tense form you do not recognize, look up the verb before assuming it is wrong — Yiddish has many legitimate irregular forms. Conversely, if you suspect an error, the dictionary will quickly confirm it.

Pay special attention to verbs that undergo a vowel change in the past tense (e.g., שרייבן → געשריבן, לויפן → גלאפן), as these are the forms most likely to be written incorrectly by analogy with regular verbs.
