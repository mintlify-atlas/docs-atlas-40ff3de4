---
title: "Yiddish Adjectives: Agreement and Verbal Adjectives"
description: "How Yiddish adjectives modify nouns, which agreement endings they take, and how past-tense verb forms function as adjectives in everyday speech."
---

Adjectives — **אדיעקטיוון** — are words that describe a property of a noun. In Yiddish, adjectives agree with the nouns they modify by taking specific endings, and almost any verb's past-tense form can double as an adjective.

## How adjectives modify nouns

An adjective appears before the noun it modifies and takes an ending that agrees with the noun's gender, number, and grammatical role. A sample of Yiddish adjectives in use:

> **אפענע** טירן, א **גוטער** דאקטאר, א **גיכע** ערהוילונג, **דינע** מצות, א **טיפער** רעיון, **ליבע** פריינד, דער **נייער** ספר, א **ענגע** קבוצה, א **פעסטער** רצון

Color names are also adjectives:

> א **רויט** לעמפל, **גרינע** גראז, **שווארצע** וואלקענעס, **ווייסע** זאקן, א **געלער** טלית

## Identifying an adjective

The defining test for an adjective is that you can add one of the following endings directly to the word — without any additional suffix — and it will still make sense:

> **－ע, －ער, －ערע, －ערער**

For example: **ריין → ריינע, ריינער, ריינערע, ריינערער**.

If you can attach one of these endings directly to a word, it is an adjective.

<Note>
If you need to add a separate suffix like **－יג** before the adjective ending can be applied, you are creating a new word, not identifying a dual-class word. **אמאל** (adverb) becomes **אמאליג** (a new adjective) through **－יג** — these are two grammatically separate words sharing a root.
</Note>

## Agreement endings

| Ending | When it is used |
|--------|----------------|
| **－ע** | Plural, feminine, and neuter in many contexts |
| **－ער** | Masculine singular (nominative) |
| **－ערע** | Comparative with plural or feminine agreement |
| **－ערער** | Comparative masculine singular |

For example, **ריין** inflects as follows:

| Form | Example |
|------|---------|
| **ריין** | base / predicate form |
| **ריינע** | **ריינע** הענט (plural); **ריינע** שטוב (feminine) |
| **ריינער** | א **ריינער** טיש (masculine singular) |
| **ריינערע** | comparative plural / feminine |
| **ריינערער** | comparative masculine singular |

## Adjectives also function as adverbs

Nearly every adjective can also function as an adverb, describing how a verb's action is performed. You use the same base form of the adjective without any ending:

> א **גוטער** דאקטאר (adjective modifying a noun) — **גוט** פארשטיין (adverb modifying a verb)

Words that can only modify verbs — and cannot modify nouns — are called pure **אדווערבן** (adverbs): דא, דארט, באלד, פלוצלינג. Words that work in both roles are called adjectives.

## Past-tense verb forms as adjectives

Almost every verb's past-tense form can serve as an adjective. You recognize these forms by the **גע-** prefix and the **-ט** or **-ן** ending typical of Yiddish past tenses:

> **געקומענע** געסט, **געזאגטע** ווערטער, **געבויטע** הייזער, **פארברענטע** ביכער, **באצאלטע** חשבונות, **בארואיגטע** עלטערן

Because the past-tense form behaves as an adjective, it takes the same agreement endings: **א געקומענער**, **א פארברענטע**, **פארברענטע הייזער**, and so on.

### Adjectives more commonly known than their source verbs

Some past-tense adjectives are so well established that they are more widely known than the original verb form. A clear example is **קאמפליצירט** — most Yiddish speakers know this adjective well, while the underlying verb **קאמפליצירן** (to complicate) is rarely used:

> א **קאמפליצירטע** פראגע — a complicated question

### Adjectives whose source verbs no longer exist

Some adjectives look like past-tense forms but derive from verbs that are no longer in use — or may never have existed as active verbs at all. Their origin is uncertain:

> **באוואוסט** (well-known), **באקאנט** (acquainted/familiar), **צעדרייט** (confused/mixed-up), **בא׳חנ׳ט** (charming), **בא׳טעמ׳ט** (decent/fitting), **געלינקט** (clumsy)

It is unclear whether verbs like **באוואוסן, באקאנען, צעדרייען** (in the relevant senses) ever existed, or whether these adjectives simply formed that way from the start.

### The case of שלייפן and its two adjectives

The verb **שלייפן** (to sharpen / to grind) illustrates how past-tense adjectives can diverge from the verb's current past-tense form. The older past-tense form **געשליפן** survives today only as an adjective meaning "refined" or "sharp-tongued" in a figurative sense. The current past-tense form of the verb is **געשלייפט**.

This means Yiddish now has two distinct adjectives from the same verb root:

| Form | Meaning | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **געשליפן** | figuratively refined, sharp | א **געשליפענע** צונג |
| **געשלייפט** | literally sharpened, ground | **געשלייפטע** דיאמאנטן |

<Tip>
When you encounter an unfamiliar adjective that looks like a past-tense form, check the dictionary. It may have a meaning distinct from the verb it appears to come from, or the original verb may no longer be in active use.
</Tip>
