This page addresses the foundational questions behind this handbook: does Yiddish actually have grammar rules, who has the authority to set them, and why does it matter for observant Jewish writers that their Yiddish be written well?

## Frequently asked questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Does Yiddish need grammar rules at all?">
    The most common objection to studying Yiddish grammar goes something like this: the main point is that readers understand what you wrote — so why does it matter whether you follow any particular rules?

    The answer is that grammar is a natural feature of every language, not an artificial constraint. Any native Yiddish speaker already follows grammatical rules without realizing it. For example, you instinctively say **אים** (him) and **מיך** (me) in the right contexts — you would never say "איך האב **ער** געזען" (I saw **he**) even though **ער** and **אים** refer to the same person.

    Some rules are so self-evident that no one disputes them. Others — like whether to write **מיך** or **מיר** in a given sentence — are less fixed, and even native speakers vary. This is exactly where a written standard earns its value.

    Beyond correctness, there is the question of **consistency**. When the same word appears spelled two different ways within one article, it creates friction for the reader — no matter which spelling you use. Having clear rules removes the constant need to make ad hoc decisions, and makes the writing process faster and smoother.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Who has the right to decide the rules?">
    Nobody owns Yiddish. No single authority can dictate how you speak or write, and the rules in this handbook do not claim to do so either.

    What the rules do is describe how the majority of Yiddish speakers and writers have used the language over time — and offer a consistent standard so that writers do not have to reinvent the wheel on their own. Over the years, linguists and language experts observed how fluent Yiddish speakers actually wrote and spoke, and derived rules from those patterns.

    In addition to solving the consistency problem for individual texts, a shared standard benefits the broader community of writers and readers. When everyone follows the same conventions, texts become easier to edit, publish, and read — especially for מגיהים (proofreaders) who review many different authors' work.

    Learning these rules may feel like effort at the start. But once they are internalized, they make writing significantly easier.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Is Yiddish really a language, or just a German dialect?">
    You may have heard the claim that Yiddish is "just a dialect of German" or "a mixture of Hebrew, German, and Polish." Neither description is accurate.

    Consider that Russian, Ukrainian, Polish, Czech, and Slovak are far more similar to one another than Yiddish is to German — yet no one argues those are all the same language. The vast majority of languages in the world contain vocabulary and structures drawn from other languages, and are still recognized as independent languages. English and French, German and Flemish, all borrow from one another — and no one questions their status as distinct languages.

    Yiddish is not only linguistically distinct from German: it is written in an entirely different alphabet. There is no stronger marker separating two languages than the use of a completely different writing system.

    <Tip>
      Researchers have found that many words from Middle High German — the shared ancestor of both Yiddish and modern German — have actually been **better preserved** in Yiddish than in modern German. In other words, if anything, Yiddish is in some respects the more faithful heir to that older language.
    </Tip>

    Modern Yiddish and modern German are not parent and child — they are siblings. Both descended from Middle High German and diverged over centuries into two distinct languages.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Isn't Yiddish grammar a project of the secular Yiddishist movement?">
    Some in the observant community express concern that Yiddish grammar is a product of secular Jewish nationalism — the "Yiddishists" who promoted Yiddish as a national language in the early twentieth century. Others worry that emphasizing Yiddish as a distinct language implies a kind of ethnic nationalism.

    This concern, while understandable, is historically incorrect.

    The impulse to establish a Yiddish standard long predates any secular movement. From the time Yiddish-speaking Jews began writing and printing in the language, there was an organic tendency — sometimes deliberate, sometimes unconscious — to develop consistent conventions, simply so that writers and readers could understand one another across regions.

    In the years before the Second World War, as Yiddish became increasingly used not just for speech but for serious printed works, observant Jewish writers and publishers sought to establish written standards for their own publications. This was an entirely internal Orthodox effort, driven by the practical needs of the frum community's growing print culture.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## זה קלי ואנוהו — beautifying the language of holy purposes

There is also a positive religious dimension to writing good Yiddish, beyond simply avoiding errors.

Since speaking Yiddish is, for many observant Jews, itself a fulfillment of a dimension of ובחוקותיהם לא תלכו — maintaining a distinctly Jewish cultural sphere — there is an argument that the principle of **זה קלי ואנוהו** (this is my God and I will beautify Him) applies here as well. Just as we beautify the mitzvos we perform, there is a reason to beautify the language we use for holy purposes — to make it clear, correct, and pleasant to read.

<Info>
  Rabbi Akiva Eiger זצ"ל wrote the following in a letter to his son:

  > אבקשך בני ידידי, לפקוח עין על זה שיהיה נדפס על נייר יפה דיו שחור ואותיות נאותות, כי לדעתי הנפש מתפעלת והדעת מתרווחת והכוונה מתעוררת מתוך הלימוד בספר נאה ומהודר, והיפוכו בהפכו אם הכתב מטושטש.

  "I ask you, my dear son, to take care that [the book] be printed on fine paper, with black ink and proper letters — for in my opinion, the soul is stirred, the mind is expanded, and intention is awakened through learning from a beautiful and well-produced book; and the opposite is true when the writing is blurred."

  Rabbi Akiva Eiger was asking his son to print his books on beautiful paper, with good ink and fine type — because the physical quality of the text affects how the reader learns from it.
</Info>

The same principle applies to the words themselves. Today it is standard practice to invest significant resources in the graphic design and layout of a sefer or a pamphlet. It is no less fitting to invest the minimal effort required to write those words correctly — especially now that Yiddish is used not only for newspapers and secular material, but for Torah works and other holy purposes.

## Shlomo Birnbaum and the pre-war Orthodox standard

<Note>
  The most significant figure in the history of Orthodox Yiddish grammar standards is **ר׳ שלמה בירנבוים ע"ה** — Shlomo Birnbaum — a son of the well-known baal teshuva Reb Nasan Birnbaum, who was held in high esteem by gedolei Yisroel.
</Note>

Shlomo Birnbaum was a linguist and researcher in Poland who wrote multiple books and articles on Yiddish grammar and spelling. He eventually formulated a complete standard specifically suited to Torah-observant Jews. His rules were taught in all Beis Yaakov schools in Poland until the Holocaust destroyed the majority of European Jewry.

His approach was grounded in **classical Yiddish** as found in older texts. For example, he favored using a י (yud) rather than an ע (ayin) in words such as **גיווען**, **גיזען**, **גיזאגט**, and **פארהאנין** — preserving older forms that reflect the historical roots of the language.

Tragically, Birnbaum's standards were largely forgotten after the war. The Orthodox community had to rebuild from the ground up, and standardizing written Yiddish was not the first priority.

## The ongoing effort to rebuild a standard

After the war, a new process of standardization gradually began among observant Yiddish writers — and it continues to this day. Writers, editors, and publishers have been working, largely without a shared written reference, to establish consistent conventions for their publications.

This handbook is a contribution to that ongoing effort. It draws on historical precedent, observed usage among serious Yiddish writers, and the principles established by earlier scholars like Birnbaum — and presents them in a clear, accessible form so that any writer who wishes to can use them.
