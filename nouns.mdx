---
title: "Yiddish Nouns: Articles, Gender, and Plural Forms"
description: "A guide to Yiddish nouns — how articles signal gender and case, how plurals are formed, diminutives, and the feminine suffix ־ין."
---

Nouns — **זאכווערטער** (also called **סובסטאנטיוון** or **אביעקטן**) — are the names of things, people, and other living beings. They are one of the most fundamental word classes in Yiddish and come with their own system of articles, plural forms, and gender marking.

## Articles: דער, די, דאס, דעם

Every Yiddish noun is used with an **ארטיקל** (article). Articles are divided into two groups:

**באשטימטע ארטיקלען** (definite articles — the equivalent of Hebrew's ה׳ הידוע):

> דער, די, דאס, דעם

**אומבאשטימטע ארטיקלען** (indefinite articles):

> אַ, אַן, קיין

The definite article you use depends on the **grammatical gender** of the noun and its role in the sentence.

| Article | Gender / Case | Example |
|---------|---------------|---------|
| **דער** | Masculine (nominative) | **דער** טאטע, **דער** טיש, **דער** זיידע |
| **די** | Feminine | **די** מאמע, **די** גאס, **די** האנט |
| **דאס** | Neuter | **דאס** הויז, **דאס** בוך, **דאס** בענקל |
| **דעם** | Masculine (accusative / dative) | **דעם** טאטן, **דעם** טיש |

<Info>
**דעם** is the accusative and dative form of the masculine definite article. Feminine and neuter articles do not change in the accusative. Full rules for when to use each form are covered in the dedicated chapter on case.
</Info>

The indefinite article **אַ** is used before consonants (**אַ מענטש**) and **אַן** before vowels (**אַן ארבעטער**). **קיין** is the negative indefinite article, used where English would say "no" or "not a/any": **קיין מענטש**.

## How to identify a noun

The quickest test for a noun is whether it can take an article. If you can place **דער, די, דאס,** or **דעם** before a word, it is a noun. A second reliable sign is that nouns can form a plural:

> **טיש – טישן**, **מענטש – מענטשן**, **וועלט – וועלטן**, **בוך – ביכער**, **הויז – הייזער**, **גאס – גאסן**

## לשון־רבים (plural forms)

Yiddish nouns form their plurals in several ways. The plural article is always **די** regardless of the noun's singular gender:

> **דער** טיש → **די** טישן  
> **דאס** הויז → **די** הייזער  
> **די** גאס → **די** גאסן

Common plural patterns include adding **־ן** or **־ען** (מענטשן, גאסן), **־ער** with or without a vowel change (ביכער, הייזער), and **־ס** in some borrowed words. The plural form of each noun is listed in the dictionary alongside the singular.

## דימיניטיוו (diminutive forms)

Yiddish makes extensive use of the **דימיניטיוו** (diminutive) to express that something is small, endearing, or affectionate. Diminutives are formed by adding the suffix **־ל** or **־עלע** and always take the neuter article **דאס**:

> **דאס** בענקל (little bench), **דאס** טיש**ל** (little table), **דאס** קינד**עלע** (dear little child)

<Tip>
Because diminutives always take **דאס**, even nouns that are masculine or feminine in their base form become grammatically neuter in their diminutive form.
</Tip>

## פעמינין — the feminine suffix ־ין

Many Yiddish nouns have a feminine counterpart formed by adding the suffix **־ין**. This suffix is pronounced the same as **־ן** or **־ען**. The distinction matters in writing even though it sounds identical in speech:

| Form | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **קעניגין** | a queen (feminine of קעניג) |
| **קעניגן** | kings (plural of קעניג), or the verb קעניגן |

Both **קעניגין** and **קעניגן** are pronounced the same way, so context — and correct spelling — is essential for clarity.

<Warning>
Because **קעניגין** (queen) and **קעניגן** (kings / the verb) sound identical in speech, you rely on context to understand the meaning. In writing, always use the correct spelling to avoid ambiguity.
</Warning>

Other examples of the feminine suffix in action: **לערערין** (female teacher, from **לערער**), **שולמיידלין** (female pupil). The feminine form normally takes **די** as its article.

## Nouns formed from other word classes

Infinitive verb forms regularly serve as nouns and always take the neuter article **דאס**:

> **דאס** זאגן, **דאס** רעדן, **דאס** לאכן, **דאס** שטיין

Similarly, adjectives can be turned into nouns by adding the suffix **־קייט**:

> **גרויס** → **די גרויסקייט**, **ארעם** → **די ארעמקייט**, **הויך** → **די הויכקייט**

These derived words are grammatically separate nouns, not dual-class words.
