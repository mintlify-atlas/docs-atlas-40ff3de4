Punctuation in Yiddish follows many of the same conventions as the local language environment in which it is written. However, several punctuation marks are specific to Yiddish and its use of Hebrew characters, and some common typographic habits from European languages — such as italic text — are simply not used. Understanding these conventions helps you write clearly and correctly.

## Standard punctuation marks

Yiddish uses the same core punctuation marks you find in most European languages. The table below lists them with their standard Yiddish usage.

| Mark | Name | Usage |
|------|------|-------|
| `.` | Period (פונקט) | Ends a declarative or imperative sentence |
| `,` | Comma (קאמע) | Separates clauses, list items, and appositives |
| `:` | Colon (קאלאן) | Introduces a list, quotation, or explanation |
| `?` | Question mark (פרעגצייכן) | Ends a direct question |
| `!` | Exclamation point (אויסרופצייכן) | Ends an exclamatory sentence or strong emphasis |
| `"…"` | Quotation marks | Encloses direct speech or quotations |
| `-` | Hyphen (מינוס/שטריך) | Joins compound words |
| `—` | Em dash (לאנגן שטריך) | Marks a parenthetical or break in thought |

<Note>
Because Yiddish is written right to left, quotation marks and other paired symbols are mirrored relative to their position in Latin-script text. Follow the conventions of the rendering environment you are working in.
</Note>

## Emphasis: bold, not italic

A common mistake when writing Yiddish in a word processor or publishing tool is using *italic* formatting for emphasis. **Do not use italic text in Yiddish.** Italic type does not look right with Hebrew-script fonts and is not an accepted convention in Yiddish writing. If you want to emphasize a word or phrase, use **bold** instead.

<Warning>
Italic formatting is not used for emphasis in Yiddish. Always use **bold** when you need to stress a word or phrase.
</Warning>

## Gereshayim (אויבערשטרעכלעך)

**Gereshayim** (״) and the single **geresh** (׳) are punctuation marks that come from Hebrew typography. They look similar to double and single apostrophes but are distinct Unicode characters: geresh is `׳` (U+05F3) and gereshayim is `״` (U+05F4). In Yiddish writing, you will encounter them in two main contexts: abbreviations and acronyms, and numbers.

### Abbreviations and acronyms

Hebrew and Aramaic abbreviations and acronyms appear frequently in Yiddish text, especially in religious and traditional writing. The convention is:

- For a **multi-letter abbreviation or acronym**, place gereshayim (״) before the last letter.
- For a **single-letter abbreviation**, place a single geresh (׳) after the letter.

| Example | Full form | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| ב״ה | בעזרת השם | Common opener meaning "with God's help" |
| אד״ג | און דעם גלייכן | Equivalent to "etc." or "and the like" |
| א״א | אונטער אנדערעם | "Among other things" |
| ר׳ | רבי / רב | Title before a name |

<Tip>
When writing abbreviations that your readers will immediately recognize — such as ב״ה or אד״ג — you do not need to spell them out. For less common abbreviations, consider spelling out the full form on first use.
</Tip>

### Numbers

Gereshayim is also used when writing numbers in Hebrew letters (as is traditional in religious texts and dates). The same rule applies: gereshayim before the last letter in a multi-character number, and geresh after a single letter.

For example, the Hebrew year ה׳תשפ״ו uses geresh after the millennium letter and gereshayim before the final letter of the year.

<Note>
In general Yiddish prose, Arabic numerals are standard. Hebrew-letter numbers with gereshayim appear mainly in religious contexts, dates in the Hebrew calendar, and quotations from sacred texts.
</Note>

## Punctuation and the local language environment

Yiddish has been written in many countries and communities, and its punctuation conventions have absorbed influences from German, Polish, Russian, and English, depending on the community. For most practical writing today, follow the standard conventions described above. Where a specific style guide applies to your publication or community, defer to that guide.
